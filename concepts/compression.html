All Concepts

Compression

Controlling dynamic range — how loud and quiet relate

Dynamics

A compressor turns down the loud parts of a signal so the difference between loud and quiet shrinks. Once you've tamed the peaks you can raise the whole thing back up (make-up gain), so the quiet detail comes forward and the overall sound feels fuller, punchier and more controlled.

Visualize

The transfer curve

Input level (x) vs. output level (y). Below the threshold the line is 1:1. Above it, the slope flattens by the ratio — that's gain reduction.

Controls

What each knob does

Threshold

The level where compression kicks in. Only signal above it gets turned down — lower threshold = more of the signal is compressed.

Ratio

How hard you turn it down above the threshold. 4:1 means 4 dB in → 1 dB out. High ratios (10:1+) act like a limiter.

Attack

How fast it clamps down once you cross the threshold. Fast = catches transients/snappy peaks; slow = lets the initial punch through.

Release

How fast it lets go after the signal drops back below threshold. Too fast can "pump"; too slow stays clamped and chokes the groove.

Knee

How abruptly compression engages around the threshold. A soft knee eases in gently and transparently; a hard knee grabs suddenly.

Make-up Gain

Since you turned the peaks down, the whole signal is quieter. Make-up gain raises it back so the now-controlled signal feels louder and fuller.

Make-up Gain

Getting the level back

Compression only ever turns things down — so after squashing the peaks, the whole signal is quieter. Make-up gain (sometimes "output" or "gain") raises everything back up. Because the loud peaks were tamed, you can push the average level higher than before without clipping, so the result sounds louder, denser and more present.

Unity / gain matching

To judge compression fairly, match the bypassed and compressed levels by ear (or meter). A rough starting point: add roughly half your gain reduction back as make-up (e.g. ~6 dB of reduction → ~3 dB make-up).

The "louder = better" trap

Make-up gain makes the signal louder, and louder almost always seems better. Always A/B at matched loudness (use Bypass below) so you're judging the compression, not just the volume bump.

Auto make-up

Many compressors offer auto make-up that estimates the level lost and adds it back automatically. Convenient, but it can over-restore on dense material — trust your ears over the auto setting.

Where it sits

Make-up gain is the last stage, after gain reduction. It doesn't change how the compressor reacts (threshold/ratio do that) — it only restores output level afterward.

Multiband

Dual-band & multiband compression

A normal ("full-band") compressor reacts to the whole signal at once — so a loud kick drum can duck the vocal, or a harsh cymbal can pump the whole mix. A multiband compressor first splits the audio into frequency bands with crossovers, then compresses each band independently and recombines them. Dual-band is the simplest case: just low and high, split at one crossover frequency.

Why split bands?

So loud lows don't trigger gain reduction on the highs (and vice-versa). You can tame a boomy low end without dulling the top, or control sibilance up top without squashing the body.

Crossover frequency

The frequency where the signal is divided into bands. For dual-band, a crossover around 150–300 Hz separates the bass/kick region from everything above it.

Per-band controls

Each band gets its own threshold, ratio, attack/release and make-up. You might gently glue the lows (slow, low ratio) while taming peaks in the highs (faster, higher ratio).

Common uses

Mastering & bus glue, de-essing (compress only the sibilance band), controlling a wandering low end, or evening out a mix where one frequency range keeps jumping out.

Watch out: independent bands can smear transients and shift tone if pushed hard. Reach for it surgically — full-band compression is still the right tool most of the time.
