Chase Bliss CXM 1978

Vintage digital studio reverb — how it works

Reverb

The CXM 1978 (a Chase Bliss × Meris collaboration) recreates the lush, dimensional sound of a late-'70s high-end digital rack reverb — think Lexicon 224, the "Blade Runner" sound. It offers three studio programs (Hall, Room, Plate), a distinctive modulated tail, and a Clock control that dials in the grit of vintage converters. Lush, smooth, and unmistakably "expensive digital."

Program

Three studio reverbs

After the dry hit comes the pre-delay gap, then the decaying tail — wavering gently when Modulation is up.

Controls

What each control does

Type (Hall / Room / Plate)

Picks the studio algorithm. Hall = big & lush, Room = tighter & more natural, Plate = smooth, dense & bright — the classic vocal/snare sound.

Time

Decay length (RT60). Short for ambience and slap, long for cavernous, evolving tails that ring for many seconds.

Pre-Delay

The gap before the reverb blooms. Keeps the dry source clear and upfront while the tail sits behind it — essential for big-but-defined sounds.

Tone

A tilt/filter on the tail. Darker for warm, distant spaces; brighter for airy, present halls. Tames or opens up the reverb's color.

Modulation

Gently detunes/chouruses the tail — the signature lush, dimensional shimmer of vintage Lexicon-style units. Subtle = movement; high = watery, dreamy.

Clock

Sets the virtual sample rate / converter quality. Full = clean & modern; lowered = darker, grittier, aliased — the authentic late-'70s lo-fi digital character.

Listen

A convolution reverb built live from your Type, Time, Tone, Modulation & Clock settings, with a modulated, optionally lo-fi tail. Mute / Panic silences everything instantly.
