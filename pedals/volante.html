Strymon Volante

Magnetic Multi-Head Echo Machine — how it works

Echo

The Volante recreates classic magnetic echo machines (think Binson Echorec). A single record head lays your signal onto a virtual loop of tape or drum, and four playback heads read it back at different points — producing multiple echoes at once. Turn on more heads and you build rhythmic patterns; feed them back and the echoes regenerate.

The Heads

One record head, four playback heads

The dot orbits the loop = the playhead. As it passes the REC head your note is written; each lit playback head reads it back later, creating an echo.

Each head has its own Echo on/off, Level, Pan, and Repeats (whether that head feeds back into the loop to regenerate).

Delay

How delay works with the heads

Time sets the gap between the record head and the furthest playback head (Head 4). The other heads sit at fractions of this distance.

Speed scales the whole loop (faster tape = shorter echoes). Spacing nudges the heads from evenly-spaced toward the irregular gaps of vintage machines.

Tone

Mechanism & character

Sets how hard you hit the virtual record head. Push it past ~60% and the media starts to saturate and soft-clip — that warm, gritty overdrive is the heart of the Volante's sound. Higher Type warmth = grit comes sooner.

Pro Tips

Getting the most out of it

Drive the Record Level

The single most "vibe" control. Low settings stay clean and hi-fi; push it past ~60% and the virtual media starts to saturate and soft-clip, adding warmth, compression and grit. Because repeats are re-recorded through this stage, each echo gets progressively more saturated — a built-in overdrive that grows as the delay decays. Pair a hot Record Level with the Drum mechanism for the thickest, most overdriven tone.

Repeats & self-oscillation

Repeats sets how much of each "feedback" head returns to the record head. Around the top of its range the repeats build instead of decaying — the machine begins to self-oscillate into runaway, swelling drones. Combined with a saturated Record Level this turns into a playable, screaming texture. Roll Repeats back to tame it.

Heads = rhythm vs. ambience

Two or three evenly-spaced heads with moderate feedback give clear, musical rhythmic subdivisions. Turn on all four, raise Repeats, and add Spacing and the taps smear together into a dense, reverb-like ambient wash. The heads are your main "delay personality" control — use Level and Pan per head to shape the groove.

Mechanics + Wear = vintage realism

Mechanics adds wow, flutter, splices and crinkle — pitch wobble and instability that sells the "old machine" feel. Wear rolls off highs as the repeats age, so echoes get darker and more distant the longer they trail. A touch of each = authentic; cranked = broken-in-the-best-way.

Time, Speed & Tap

Use Tap Tempo (the footswitch on the hardware) to lock Time to the song. Switching Speed (½× / 1× / 2×) mid-phrase bends the pitch of the trails like a real tape motor — great for dives and risers. Remember Time sets Head 4; the other heads always follow as fractions of it.

SoS looper, reverb & Favorite

Beyond delay, the Volante has a Sound-on-Sound looper (with reverse, pause, splice and infinite hold) and a built-in spring reverb you can blend in for extra space. Stash a go-to setting on the Favorite switch so you can jump to it instantly mid-set. Stereo outs + per-head Pan make it a wide, immersive ambient machine.
